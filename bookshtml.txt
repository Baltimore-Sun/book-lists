When she chose to take a seat at one of the tables in the back, behind her was a high window through which she could see the turrets and rampart of Edinburgh Castle.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>“In my opinion, the best writing café is just crowded enough so that you blend in, but not so crowded that you end up sharing a table with somebody who tries to read [your work] upside down, has staff who don’t glower at you if you sit there too long . . . and doesn’t play very loud music,” described Rowling.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Her sister’s husband, Roger, had recently opened a café called Nicolson’s, and Rowling figured that she would not be as much of a nuisance there, what with her purchasing a single cup of coffee. There were “so many tables” in Nicolson’s that “I didn’t feel too guilty about taking [one] up too long,” she said. She avoided visiting her brother-in-law’s café during what she knew were their busy periods. Walking through the blue-and-yellow dining area, the walls bearing papier-mâché flowers, she took a seat at her favorite table in the corner.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Nicolson’s was housed on the second floor of a building across from the imposing, columned Old College of the University of Edinburgh. Rowling loved looking down at the street below, which was filled with traffic and pedestrians and which ended at the famous Balmoral Hotel beyond.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Knowing that additional cups might put her over the edge, she sipped a cup or two of cold espresso at her corner table and wrote in long-hand. She liked using a black ballpoint pen best if she had one. It was warm in the café, and rain sometimes pattered on the windows. Inscribing her ideas onto a piece of paper, she concentrated her mind on completing Book One while Jessica remained napping. “You can get a hell of a lot of writing done in two hours if you know that’s the only chance you are going to get,” said Rowling.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>One sunny afternoon, she ducked into Nicolson’s after Jessica dozed off, took her seat, and flew through an entire chapter of the book. She wrote what ultimately became Chapter Ten, “Halloween,” in one sitting, and unusually changed almost nothing about it after review. In the chapter, Rowling had Harry and Ron face down a troll who had trapped Hermione in the girl’s bathroom. When the boys are reprimanded by Professor McGonagall after fighting the troll by themselves, Hermione takes the blame for the incident, thereby winning the respect of Harry and Ron, who had become fed up with her bossy, know-it-all attitude. “Hermione, bless her, is so very annoying in the early part of ‘Philosopher’s Stone’ that I really felt it needed something (literally) huge to bring her together with Harry and Ron,” commented Rowling.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>According to Nicolson’s general manager Roland Thomson, the staff at Nicolson’s all knew “Joanne and Jessica.”</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>“They would come in almost every day, and the wee girl would sleep while her mother wrote,” he said. Rowling appreciated the friendly and patient staff members at Nicolson’s, and she joked about helping to make the café famous if her book got picked up. “Well, you know, [if ] it gets published, I’ll try and get you loads of publicity,” she would humorously declare.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Apart from when she was experiencing one of her especially grim moments, Rowling passionately believed in her book, at times heading home down Leith Walk feeling elated at the writing she had churned out that day. “People would ask me what I had been doing and I would say just out walking,” said Rowling. “I think they thought I was very strange and possibly depressed.”</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>One time, after a caffeine-fueled writing session, she heard a seemingly prophetic voice speak inside her head while on the North-South Bridges near Nicolson’s. “The difficulty will be in getting this published,” the voice told her. “But if it’s published, there will be a handful of people who really love it.”</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>“Oh come on!” Rowling internally replied to the voice in disbelief. She snapped back into outer reality on the Bridges, nearby the Balmoral Hotel. The hotel was a luxurious, Victorian-era establishment with a silver stone clocktower—where the rich could rest their heads for one night at a sum that was greater than the amount Rowling and Jessica lived on for one week through welfare.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>While scraping by and feeling anger over her many failures, Rowling said that she felt like a “waste of space” of a human being.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Joanne Rowling certainly took up space (however small). She took up a unit in a block of apartment buildings and utilized a bed to sleep next to her child when night came on. She covered her body with clothes obtained from a thrift shop. In order to continue existing, she consumed precious supplies of air and food. She used water to wash herself and required diapers to cover her impoverished baby’s bottom. Rowling placed her feet inside a pair of black boots with laces when she decided to go out and step on the city pavement on the way to a café. And at these cafés, she topped it all by using up a coffee, table, and piece of paper while sitting and engaging in her pastime of writing a story about a boy who goes to magic school. (She also took up space in public bathrooms, once jotting a paragraph of Harry Potter while crouching on the public toilet.)</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Twenty-nine-year-old, red-haired, blue-eyed Joanne Rowling was one of billions of insignificants. She could make no fashion statement—people would have given no glance at her clothing and attire. She would have known that any makeup or jewelry or accessories she might don, whether the eyeliner or dangly earrings she enjoyed wearing, would not turn her into a person important enough to make a fashion statement (and, of course, she could not don those articles even if she wanted to, what with her dependence on secondhand clothes). She would have understood that most of the people walking by her on the street did not care about her opinions on politics or the economy or the state of the world they shared. When she died, she would join the ranks of dispensable people interred in cemeteries.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>But Rowling had her two babies with her: Jessica, “whom I adored,” she said, and her beloved book—“my baby,” as she described it once. Suspecting that she knew how to tell a story, if nothing else, she committed herself to doing right by Harry Potter at the least.</p></div><br><br><br>
