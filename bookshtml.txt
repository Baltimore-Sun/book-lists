This is Beckett before the fasting cure, before the scalpel-clean desolations of <em>Godot</em> and <em>Endgame</em>—young, horny, overread, verbally incontinent, and gloriously unwilling to leave any sentence unperfumed. Belacqua, that proto-Beckett buffoon-prince, moons over women, language, and his own exquisitely upholstered suffering while the book pirouettes between lyric rapture and collegiate smirk. At times it feels less like a novel than a nervous breakdown with excellent diction.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>And that is precisely the thrill. <em>Dream</em> is messy, libidinous, show-offy, intermittently sublime—the rough draft of an annihilating sensibility learning how to sing before it learned how to starve. The jokes already carry Beckett’s signature aftertaste: laughter with the blood drained from it. So does the pity. What survives the youthful excess is the unmistakable birth of a consciousness that would later strip literature to the bone. Here the bones are still hidden under velvet, perfume, and prankish erudition.
