Agricultural production underwent a rapid and widespread shift from traditional methods to reliance on new hybrid crop strains, mechanization, expanded irrigation infrastructure, and the use of chemical fertilizers, herbicides, and pesticides. These shifts profoundly increased crop yields in the short term. They lifted millions of people out of poverty, but over time, productivity has declined as the hidden environmental and health costs of these practices have become evident.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>The benefits of industrial food production were soon visible—increased yields of cereal crops supplied meals to millions and allowed many nations to become net food exporters rather than importers, thereby boosting their economies and food security simultaneously. Poverty was widely eradicated, real incomes rose, infant mortality rates declined, and more food was grown on less land. The putative “father” of the Green Revolution, Norman Borlaug, was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1970, credited with saving over a billion human lives.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>The modern world of agricultural technology is truly astounding. Genetic engineering, hormones, antibiotics, and other scientific advances have joined a fossil-fuel-powered array of mechanical marvels that surpass the wildest imaginings of science fiction. Magnificent computer-guided tractors with sophisticated attachments plow the ground, seed vast regions, spray fertilizers, insecticides, and specially formulated chemicals, and harvest a bounty unimaginable a century ago. Specialty machines have been designed for harvesting corn, wheat, berries, watermelons, and greens—a world of technology nearly as varied as the crops available to grow. Irrigation, processing, and distribution networks similarly impress the human imagination.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>But this fantastic bounty comes with a tremendous cost. Industrially tilled soils are sickly and eroding. Precious underground water aquifers have been slurped up in dry regions, evaporating rapidly on dying soils. Chemical applications have sickened the soil microbiome, depleted vital minerals, and killed pollinators.5 Human health is deteriorating as man-made food products tainted with chemicals and deprived of nutritional value have replaced natural provisions.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>As is so often the case with technological innovation, the downsides of this revolution were not immediately evident. Because the chief motivation of agricultural conversion was to fill empty stomachs, food quality and ecosystem health were not prioritized. Modern crops, especially those reliant on GMO-dependent monocultures, including tens of millions of acres of corn and soy, rely heavily on synthetic fertilizers, which deplete soils and water resources. The chemical applications (especially pesticides, glyphosate, and herbicides) upon which they are dependent inflict ecological destruction.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>The ugly truth of the Green Revolution is that seemingly miraculous productivity gains have masked numerous problems that far outweigh the supposed benefits. Compacted soils reduce tilth and yield; chemical applications weaken soils, accelerating erosion and necessitating ever-greater applications of fertilizers and other chemicals. Roughly half of the world’s food production is now dependent on synthetic nitrogen fertilizer use.7</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Genetic modifications permitted an even greater extension of these perceived production benefits, adding glyphosate as a chemical dependency to the supposedly salvific industrial food production system. America industry, university researchers, and government agencies all pushed US food production toward greater “efficiency” and yields, sacrificing crop biodiversity and local agrarian cultures on the altars of profit and technological progress. Large-scale agricultural production increased farm consolidation. Only larger enterprises had access to credit (often from foreign investors) and could take advantage of bulk purchasing power.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>The consolidation of food production, processing, and distribution into an ever-shrinking club of enormously powerful corporations or massive farms diminishes local food supplies and local food security. It also centralizes food production, creating a dependency on fossil fuel energy—diesel and gasoline to power tractors and equipment, and natural gas to manufacture synthetic fertilizers.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>This arrangement worked economically as long as oil and other fuels remained cost-effective. Rising fertilizer costs (which spiked sharply during the COVID-19 pandemic) have already begun to reverse those favorable economics. Over the decades, industrial crop yields have declined, necessitating ever-higher applications of fertilizers and other inputs to maintain yields. Pesticides, herbicides, and defoliants, as well as agricultural machinery and irrigation systems, are all derived from and dependent upon crude oil for their manufacture.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Industrial fertilizers have dramatically exacerbated environmental problems, especially from synthetic urea—excess urea results in emissions of nitrous oxide, which is considered by the greenhouse gas metric to be 265 times more potent than carbon dioxide. Water quality and quantity have both suffered significantly from the Green Revolution, as chemicals pollute drinking water and aquifers are depleted far more quickly than nature can replenish them.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Corporations profited tremendously from the Green Revolution, but human and environmental health have been dangerously compromised. Commodity output and economic growth increased at the expense of soil, water, food wholesomeness, and food security. Long-standing regenerative farming practices were abandoned, chemicals were spewed recklessly, and water supplies were gobbled up shortsightedly. Unsurprisingly, yield rates have steadily declined, necessitating the use of more land, chemicals, and fossil fuels.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Inexpensive, plentiful food supplies are thus a short-term illusion, constructed atop a fragile dependency on finite energy sources that are steadily becoming more costly. The system is both economically and environmentally unsustainable, as well as unhealthy for humans and the ecosystem. Yet, this unnatural production model is enthusiastically embraced by the cadre of corporate profiteers who have led the charge to “save the world” with gimmicks and deceptions about agriculture. These same forces now conspire to pressure the entire world’s agricultural production into this toxic, food-dominating model in the name of mitigating climate change and averting global famine. This consolidation increases the long-term risks of famine.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>The greatest cost of this seemingly wondrous industrial transition remains concealed from most people’s awareness: dependency on these technologies, replacing the once-universal local connection between humans and soil. When God evicted Adam and Eve from the Garden of Eden, He left them an umbilical cord to the life-giving Earth. That nurturing connection has been effectively severed by the radical modern shift from a direct relationship with soil, plants, and animals to a dependency on cheap oil pumped from deserts or ocean floors far away, on computer chips manufactured in Taiwan, on distant, impersonal supply lines, and on processing and preservatives.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Corporate America, now sitting at the table of the World Economic Forum in partnership with the United Nations and the World Health Organization, seeks to make the final connection from its New World Order factories to our human navels. Dazzled by the technology and ease promised, humanity has taken the proverbial bait at great peril.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Once disconnected from rich soils and nutrient-dense foods, humans become not only physically ill but also alienated from other vital connections. Wendell Berry correctly observes that “As industrial technology advances and enlarges, and in the process assumes greater social, economic, and political force, it carries people away from where they belong by history, culture, deeds, association, and affection.” This severance unmoors modern human populations from their ability to reclaim their own food production, including cultural knowledge, community, rural lands, livestock, seed genetics, and the tools and other resources required to grow their own food.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>The journey to rediscover the primacy of food in human health is a homecoming to agri<em>culture</em>, its rich history, complex economics, vibrant community, and profound meaning. It is not just food that has set modern Americans adrift: it is the alienation from farming and food culture, the loss of rural communities and economic sustenance, and the abandonment of agrarian life in favor of techno-urban alienation and confinement. Most importantly, it is an utter surrender of self-determination and liberty to corporate-government domination.</p></div><br><br><br>
