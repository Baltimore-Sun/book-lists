The hive that suppresses, by definition, meaning and purpose, incubates its own destruction.<em> The Last Men: Liberalism and the Death of Masculinity </em>is a stunningly cogent education in where we are now—and a warning.</p></div><br><br><h4 style='border-top:3px solid #1c2551;padding-top:10px;'>Book of the Week</h4><a href='https://bookshop.org/a/113618/9781510786783' target='_blank'><img style='float: left;width: 120px;padding: 0 20px 20px 0;margin-top: 5px' alt='The Last Men: Liberalism and the Death of Masculinity book cover image' src='https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1NiNbnigQK0_OrmcoZGKTwHgq44buQf5G'></a><strong><a href='https://bookshop.org/a/113618/9781510786783' target='_blank' style='color:#1c2551;'>The Last Men: Liberalism and the Death of Masculinity</a></strong><br>Dr. Charles Cornish-Dale<br><em>Regnery (December 16, 2025)</em><div style='clear: both'></div><div style='position:relative;top:-22px;'><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'><strong>Dr. CHARLES CORNISH-DALE</strong> is an Oxford- and Cambridge-educated historian and anthropologist who rose to prominence writing under the pseudonym “RAW EGG NATIONALIST.” He has hundreds of thousands of social-media followers; has sold tens of thousands of books; has appeared on national television; and decisively influenced the course of US politics with his inspired leadership on health, fitness, politics, and culture.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>“If the broscientists have a spiritual leader, it would be a man who calls himself ‘Raw Egg Nationalist’”<br /> <strong>—Tucker Carlson</strong><br /> <br /> "The masculinist health guru."<br /> <strong><em>—Vanity Fair</em></strong><br /> <br /> "A puckish English reactionary."<br /> <strong>—Liz Bruenig, <em>The Atlantic</em></strong></p><h5 style="padding-bottom: 5px;border-bottom: 1px solid #000;margin-top: 25px;margin-bottom: 16px">An Excerpt from <em>The Last Men </em></h5><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Francis Fukuyama’s 1989 essay “The End of History,” and the book it spawned three years later, is remembered as a paean to the triumph of liberal capitalism at the end of the Cold War. In Fukuyama’s own words, with the fall of Communism in Europe, mankind had reached, “not just the end of the Cold War, or the passing of a particular period of post-war history, but the end of history as such: that is, the end point of mankind’s ideological evolution, and the universalization of Western liberal democracy as the final form of human government.”</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>To many after September 11, the Global War on Terror, the rise of China and the Migrant Crisis of 2015, and with the “Climate Crisis” supposedly about to make large swathes of the planet literally uninhabitable for human life, Fukuyama’s pronouncement of the End of History was premature, to say the least.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Big things are very much still happening.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Of course, if you’ve taken the time to read Fukuyama, you’ll know he wasn’t saying that history—i.e., the onward progression of time and events—had ceased. Nobody, not even a Harvard political scientist, could be that dumb. Rather, Fukuyama believed the evolution of social and political forms—a process which, like Marx and Hegel, he saw as developing in a linear fashion by means of contradictions and clashes—had finally come to an end. By defeating communism, liberal capitalist democracy had shown once and for all its superiority as a way of ordering life. Based on what we know about politics, economics, and ethics—and about man himself—nothing better could ever come along. And although there might be regressions, moments when the totalitarian impulses of the mid-20th century returned, these would always prove temporary. Liberal capitalist democracy would win again.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Always.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'><br /> <strong><em>In Search of the Last Men</em></strong></p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>The basics of Fukuyama’s thesis are apt to be misrepresented, and Fukuyama himself is often made to serve as a straw man for the Western liberal elite and its monumental hubris, but other parts of his thesis are missed altogether. The title of his book is popularly known as <em>The End of History</em>. In fact, the full title is <em>The End of History and the Last Man</em>. This omission from the general consciousness is telling. Because not only is the book a proclamation of victory, it is also a warning—a warning that takes up the final fifth of the book. A warning about the Last Man.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Who or what is this Last Man?</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>In simple terms, the Last Man is what you get at the End of History, when there’s no longer anything particularly important worth living for. When the great ethical, economic and political questions have all been settled, what higher purposes exist for men to strive towards? The answer is: none. Life becomes dull not through any individual defect but on a metaphysical level.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'><br /> <strong><em>The Changing Place of </em>Thymos</strong></p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>Megalothymia, a Greek word, is key here. <em>Thymos</em>, according to the Greeks, was the part of an individual’s soul that desires recognition or dignity. The word could be translated as something approximating “spiritedness” or “warm-bloodedness.” It’s an attribute all men possess and can develop. Fukuyama explains how Plato “compares a man with thymos to a noble dog who is capable of great courage and anger fighting strangers in defense of his own city.” <em>Thymos </em>can be equated, in some sense, with what might today be called “self-esteem.” As Fukuyama puts it, “<em>Thymos </em>is something like an innate sense of justice: people believe that they have a certain worth, and when other people act as though they are worthless—when they do not <em>recognize </em>their worth at its correct value—then they become angry.”</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'><em>Thymos</em>, then, is intimately tied to a person’s place in the social order, to his sense of self-worth (which must be reflected in others), and to his desire, ultimately, to assert his claim to these things. Fukuyama generally describes <em>thymos </em>as the “desire for recognition.”</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>The Greeks believed <em>thymos </em>came in various forms. There was <em>isothymia</em>, a desire to be acknowledged as equal to everybody else, which can be seen as the central impulse at the heart of the Christian and democratic ideals. Then there was <em>megalothymia</em>, which is the desire to seek distinction above others, in opposition to <em>isothymia</em>.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>In the liberal-democratic end-state, where all men have been decided, at last, to be equal, <em>isothymia </em>is satisfied, but <em>megalothymia </em>is not and cannot be. Ever. And that’s not good.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>In <em>Civilization and Its Discontents</em>, Sigmund Freud cautioned that when an entire population has unfulfilled or even deliberately suppressed desires, turmoil will follow. This is the crux of Fukuyama’s warning about the End of History: man’s nature cannot be denied. While it’s entirely possible that man will be happy now as one among many billions of equals, each pursuing his own limited goals like making money, buying things, having sex, and going on holidays, it’s equally possible that man—or rather <em>some </em>men—will rebel in the name of satisfying their desire to be different. To be better. What we might see instead of a future of cattle-like dolts enjoying an easy contentment is “immense wars of the spirit”—and Fukuyama means, quite literally, <em>wars</em>—with wild men letting loose and painting the world red again in the name of personal glory and some higher purpose what-ever that might be—that is, anything other than endless, monotonous peace and sex and commerce.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>This is the scenario dramatized by the French writer Michel Houellebecq in his second novel <em>Platform</em>, where a self-satisfied French sex tourist, having reconciled himself to a life of hedonism on the beaches of Thailand, is caught up in a violent Islamic terrorist attack that kills his partner and eventually drives him to suicide.</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'><em>Isothymia</em>, say hello to your old friend <em>megalothymia</em>!</p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'><br /> <strong><em>Not with a Bang but a Whimper?</em></strong></p><p style='margin-bottom:16px;'>So what began as a supposedly ringing and uncomplicated endorsement of liberal democratic capitalism by Fukuyama has ended with a dire warning about the degeneration of man in a social prison of his own making and the desperate attempts he might make to free himself from it at any cost. Whatever Fukuyama’s original intention, <em>The End of History </em>offers one of the most devastating criticisms not only of the democratic form but of the forces that are making life hostile on a planetary scale to the human spirit. Although I don’t agree that liberal democratic capitalism is the end stage of man’s social and political development, I do agree the “unipolar moment” that followed the fall of communism in Europe was a hollow victory, and that, so long as liberalism is in the ascendant, man—and specifically <em>men</em>—are going to continue to suffer from a profound crisis of meaning and purpose.</p></div><br><br><br>
